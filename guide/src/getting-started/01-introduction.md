ActOne is a narrative design language and studio for interactive storytelling. It gives writers, game designers, and world-builders a structured way to define the people, places, and events of their stories — and a visual environment in which to see those pieces come together.

Whether you are writing a branching game narrative, mapping the plot of a novel, or designing an interactive experience, ActOne provides the vocabulary and tools to organize your creative work with clarity and precision.

## What ActOne Is

At its core, ActOne is two things working together:

**The ActOne Language** is a text-based notation for describing narrative elements. You write your story in `.act` files using a clear, readable syntax. You define characters with personalities and goals, worlds with locations and rules, scenes with beats and dialogue, timelines that arrange those scenes across time, and plots that trace the arc of your narrative.

**ActOne Studio** is the editing environment that understands your language files. The Studio provides syntax highlighting and auto-complete as you type, an Outline panel that shows your story's structure as a tree, a Diagram panel that draws a visual map of how characters, worlds, and scenes relate to one another, a Problems panel that flags any inconsistencies or errors, and an Export panel that produces finished documents in DOCX and PDF formats.

Together, the language and the Studio let you move fluidly between writing prose and understanding structure — without leaving your creative flow.

## Who ActOne Is For

ActOne is built for anyone who works with structured narrative:

- **Writers and authors** who want to map the architecture of a novel, screenplay, or short story collection before drafting — or while revising.
- **Game designers and narrative designers** who need to define characters, worlds, branching scenes, and player interactions for video games, tabletop RPGs, or interactive fiction.
- **World-builders** who are developing rich fictional settings with many locations, factions, timelines, and relationships that need to stay consistent across a large body of work.
- **Story teams** working collaboratively on a shared narrative universe, where multiple people need to reference and extend a common set of characters and events.

You do not need to be a programmer to use ActOne. The language reads almost like structured prose, and the Studio is designed to give you instant visual feedback on what you have written.

## Core Strengths

ActOne excels in several areas that are difficult to manage with ordinary word processors or spreadsheets:

**Structured characters.** Every character you define carries consistent traits — nature (protagonist, antagonist, and so on), biography, role in the story, voice, personality, goals, and relationships to other characters.

**Connected worlds.** Worlds contain locations, and those locations can appear in scenes. The Diagram panel shows you which characters inhabit which worlds and where scenes take place.

**Scene-level precision.** Scenes are first-class elements with settings, casts, and beats. A beat is the smallest dramatic unit — a revelation, a confrontation, a quiet moment — and ActOne gives each beat its own structure.

**Temporal layering.** Timelines let you place scenes across layers — past, present, future, or parallel — so you can reason about flashbacks, flash-forwards, and branching paths without losing track of where things stand.

**Narrative arcs.** Plots connect scenes into arcs with turning points and resolutions, giving your story's structure a shape you can inspect and adjust.

**Interactive choice points.** Interactions define the moments where your audience makes a choice. ActOne tracks the options, conditions, and consequences of each interaction, which is essential for game narratives and interactive fiction.

## How This Guide Is Organized

This guide is arranged in four sections:

**Getting Started** (you are here) introduces ActOne, explains its core concepts, walks through the Studio interface, and guides you through creating your first story. Start here if you are new to ActOne.

**User Guide** goes deeper into everyday workflows: creating and managing projects, using the editor efficiently, working with multi-file projects, reading diagrams, and exporting your work.

**Language Reference** is a complete reference for every element type, keyword, attribute, and validation rule in the ActOne language. Return to this section when you need to look up exact syntax or understand what a specific construct does.

**Reference** contains a glossary of ActOne terms and a troubleshooting guide for common issues.
